What is Autism?

Clinically known as Autism Spectrum Condition according to the DSM-5

"Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a neurological and developmental disorder that affects how people interact with others, communicate, learn, and behave. Although autism can be diagnosed at any age, it is described as a 'developmental disorder' because symptoms generally appear in the first 2 years of life."

(National Institute of Mental Health, 2022)

It affects interaction, communication, learning and behaviour.
Can be diagnosed at any age but symptoms tend to appear in early life, therefore described as a developmental disorder.

Autism is a part of neurodiversity.

Autism is a Part of Neurodiversity

Neurodiversity is defined as:

"the range of differences in individual brain function and behavioural traits, regarded as part of normal variation in the human population"

Autism is not a linear spectrum but rather made up of many aspects that are affected differently.

What is Sensory Processing?

Sensory input is captured via receptors:

External Stimuli:

  • Auditory
  • Visual
  • Tactile
  • Olfactory
  • Gustatory

Internal Stimuli:

  • Proprioceptive
  • Vestibular
  • Interoceptive
  • The input is then transformed into sensory information.
  • The sensory information is then sent to the brain to be processed and organised.
  • The brain then determines a motor and behavioural response.

(Galiana-Simal et al., 2020)

Input is transformed into sensory information that is sent to the brain, which determines motor and behavioural responses.

Perceptual Experience

Perceptual experience is influenced by:

  • Incoming sensory information
  • Prior knowledge held about the world

Formed using Bayesian decision theory:

Predicts an outcome based on previous observations along with information from the current situation

Can be used to show the differences in perceptual mechanisms in autistic people.

Pellicano and Burr's account assumes that perceptual atypicalities occur because autistic people are less influenced by their prior knowledge.

(Pellicano and Burr, 2012)

What are Sensory Differences?

Sensory differences can be different experiences with our senses:

  • Sight
  • Hearing
  • Smell
  • Taste
  • Touch
  • Proprioception (bodily awareness)
  • Vestibular (balance)

These experiences can change interactions and how someone physically feels.

Sensory difference are not specific to autistic people, people with ADHD, OCD or developmental delays can have them too.

Sensory Differences in Autistic People

He et al.'s Taxonomy

He et al., developed a taxonomy that differentiates between levels of sensory differences experienced by autistic people.

(He et al., 2023)

Dunn's Model of Sensory Processing

Dunn proposed a model pertaining to 4 basic patterns of sensory processing which come from the neurological threshold and self-regulation.

(Dunn's Model of Sensory Processing | OT Theory, 2018)

Sensory Reactivity Differences in Autistic People

Sensory differences are experienced by 65%-94% of autistic people

(Crane et al 2009; Tavassoli et al, 2016)

Some experiences are enjoyable whereas some are extremely distressing (this can impact mental and physical health).

(MacLennan, O'Brien et al., 2021)

  • 94% experience hyperreactivity
  • 29% experience hyporeactivity
  • 41% experience seeking
  • Overlapping hyperreactivity and seeking most common
  • Overlapping hyporeactivity and seeking least common

(MacLennan, O'Brien et al., 2021)

Hyperreactivity is showing high sensitivity to a stimulus
Hyporeactivity is being unresponsive to a stimulus
Seeking is the search for the experiences and feelings in response to a stimulus

Anxiety

"Anxiety is a feeling of unease, such as worry or fear, that can be mild or severe. Everyone has feelings of anxiety at some point in their life. For example, you may feel worried and anxious about sitting an exam or having a medical test or job interview."

(NHS Inform, 2022)

  • It does not go away
  • Can gradually get worse with time
  • Interferes with everyday life

Anxiety is the main symptom of several conditions, including:

  • Panic Disorder
  • Phobias, such as agoraphobia or claustrophobia
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
  • Social anxiety disorder (social anxiety)

(NHS, 2018)

Anxiety and Autism

Research suggests that anxiety is more common in autistic people.

(Nimmo-Smith et al., 2020; Lugnegård et al., 2011; Van Steensel et al., 2011)

Autistic people are also known to show unique anxiety conditions alongside the traditional anxiety presentations:

Kerns et al., 2014

Found that 63% of ASD youth presented with impairing anxiety:

  • 31% with both traditional and atypical anxiety
  • 17% with traditional anxiety only
  • 15% with atypical anxiety only

Unique anxiety presentations include:

  • Sensory fears
  • Less common phobias
  • Social fears unrelated to negative evaluation

Research into the Sensory Differences and Links to Anxiety in Autistic People

Research into the anxiety and possible sensory triggers in autistic people can help us to understand and research causes of autism and intervention treatment.

Most research focuses on Sensory Over-Reactivity in autistic people, but I want to focus on research that looks into all the different types of sensory reactivity differences.

This approach is more beneficial and progressive than only focusing on hyperreactivity.

MacLennan et al., 2020

The Relationship Between Sensory Reactivity Differences and Anxiety Subtypes in Autistic Children

The study used 41 autistic children (who all had a clinical diagnosis) aged 3–14 using parent and self-report measures to report anxiety and sensory differences.

Findings:

  • Sensory hyperreactivity was related to phobia-related symptoms
  • Sensory hyporeactivity was related to social anxiety
  • Sensory seeking was not related to anxiety

Positives:

  • Insight into the relationship between sensory reactivity differences and anxiety subtypes
  • Uses standardised measures to assess sensory reactivity and anxiety subtypes
  • Found significant relationships which help contribute to more specific interventions
  • Highlights the importance of considering the sensory reactivity differences in diagnosis and treatment

Negatives:

  • Small sample size limits generalisability
  • Relied on parent and self-report (could be biased or not accurately reflect the child's experiences)
  • Did not examine other contributing factors such as social communication difficulties or executive functioning deficits

Why is This a Good Study?

  • It is a valid and reliable study as it uses standardized measures
  • Sheds light on a topic that hasn't been extensively researched
  • Has important implications for future research
  • Can assist with the development of interventions tailored to specific sensory needs

However, this study only focuses on children…

Verhulst et al., 2022

The Perceived Causal Relations Between Sensory Reactivity Differences and Anxiety Symptoms in Autistic Adults

The study used 246 autistic adults aged 18–76 in an online study.
Used self-report assessments of sensory reactivity differences and anxiety, followed by a perceived causal relations scale to indicate whether they thought their sensory reactivity differences were more of a cause or an effect of their anxiety symptoms.

Findings:

  • All types of sensory reactivity were correlated with anxiety
  • Hyperreactivity was perceived to be a cause rather than an effect
  • Seeking was perceived to be an effect rather than a cause

Positives:

  • One of the first studies to explore perceived causal relationships between reactivity differences and anxiety
  • Self-report allows for direct assessment of participants' subjective experiences
  • Large sample size enhances generalisability
  • Suggests cause vs. effect insight, with positive implications for assessment and treatment

Negatives:

  • Self-report can introduce bias
  • Did not examine other contributing factors such as executive function or social support
  • Did not include a control group of non-autistic people for comparison

Why is This a Good Study?

  • Addresses important research that can be used to develop effective treatments for this demographic
  • Uses reliable and valid self-report measures
  • Large sample size increases generalisability
  • Statistical analysis strengthens validity
  • Adds to the limited research in this area with implications for assessment, research, and treatment

Final Thoughts

There are many studies that look into the relationship between sensory differences and anxiety in autistic people.
I think these two studies are the most useful in understanding the direction of research, whereas earlier studies tend to focus only on hyperreactivity and sensory over-reactivity.

These papers explore the variations in sensory reactivity.

A lot more research needs to be done, as these studies have limitations. However, it seems that understanding the relationship between sensory processing differences and anxiety in autistic people is key to developing effective interventions.

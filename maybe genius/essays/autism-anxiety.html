01. What is Autism?

Clinically known as Autism Spectrum Condition according to the DSM-5:

"Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a neurological and developmental disorder that affects how people interact with others, communicate, learn, and behave. Although autism can be diagnosed at any age, it is described as a 'developmental disorder' because symptoms generally appear in the first 2 years of life." (National Institute of Mental Health, 2022)
  • Affects interaction, communication, learning, and behaviour
  • Can be diagnosed at any age
  • Symptoms tend to appear in early life

Autism is a part of neurodiversity.

Autism is a Part of Neurodiversity

Neurodiversity is defined as:

"The range of differences in individual brain function and behavioural traits, regarded as part of normal variation in the human population."

Autism is not a linear spectrum but made up of many aspects affected differently.

02. What is Sensory Processing?

Sensory input is captured via receptors:

External Stimuli:

  • Auditory
  • Visual
  • Tactile
  • Olfactory
  • Gustatory

Internal Stimuli:

  • Proprioceptive
  • Vestibular
  • Interoceptive
  • Input is transformed into sensory information
  • Sent to the brain to be processed and organised
  • Brain determines motor and behavioural responses

(Galiana-Simal et al., 2020)

03. Sensory Perception

Perceptual experience is influenced by:

  • Incoming sensory information
  • Prior knowledge of the world

Formed using Bayesian Decision Theory:

  • Predicts outcomes based on past observations and current inputs
  • Explains perceptual differences in autistic individuals

Pellicano and Burr (2012) suggest autistic people are less influenced by prior knowledge.

04. What are Sensory Differences?

Sensory differences refer to varied sensory experiences:

  • Sight
  • Hearing
  • Smell
  • Taste
  • Touch
  • Proprioception
  • Vestibular

These differences:

  • Influence interactions and physical feelings
  • Are not exclusive to autism (can occur in ADHD, OCD, developmental delays)

05. Sensory Differences in Autistic People

He et al.'s Taxonomy

A taxonomy that differentiates levels of sensory differences in autistic people.

(He et al., 2023)

Dunn's Model of Sensory Processing

Outlines 4 basic sensory patterns based on neurological threshold and self-regulation.

(Dunn's Model of Sensory Processing | OT Theory, 2018)

Sensory Reactivity Differences in Autism

  • 65%-94% of autistic people experience sensory differences
  • Experiences may be enjoyable or distressing

(MacLennan, O'Brien et al., 2021)

Types:

  • Hyperreactivity: high sensitivity
  • Hyporeactivity: unresponsive to stimuli
  • Seeking: active pursuit of sensory input

Prevalence:

  • 94% hyperreactivity
  • 29% hyporeactivity
  • 41% seeking
  • Most common: overlapping hyperreactivity and seeking
  • Least common: overlapping hyporeactivity and seeking

06. Anxiety

"Anxiety is a feeling of unease, such as worry or fear, that can be mild or severe..." (NHS Inform, 2022)
  • Persistent and may worsen over time
  • Can interfere with daily life

Common anxiety disorders:

  • Panic Disorder
  • Phobias (agoraphobia, claustrophobia)
  • PTSD
  • Social Anxiety Disorder

07. Anxiety in Autism

Research shows anxiety is more common in autistic people.

(Nimmo-Smith et al., 2020; Lugnegård et al., 2011; Van Steensel et al., 2011)

Kerns et al. (2014):

  • 63% of autistic youth had impairing anxiety
    • 31% traditional + atypical
    • 17% traditional only
    • 15% atypical only

Unique anxiety presentations in autism:

  • Sensory fears
  • Uncommon phobias
  • Social fears not linked to negative evaluation

08. Research

Research into sensory triggers and anxiety in autism:

  • Helps develop better understanding and targeted interventions
  • Most past studies focus only on hyperreactivity

MacLennan et al., 2020

Study on autistic children (n=41, aged 3–14):

Findings:

  • Hyperreactivity linked to phobia symptoms
  • Hyporeactivity linked to social anxiety
  • Seeking not linked to anxiety

Strengths:

  • Standardised measures
  • Found significant relationships
  • Helps tailor interventions

Limitations:

  • Small sample size
  • Reliance on self/parent reports
  • Did not consider other factors like executive function

Conclusion:

  • Valid and reliable study
  • Contributes to research and interventions
  • Limited to children

Verhulst et al., 2022

Study on autistic adults (n=246, ages 18–76):

Methods:

  • Online self-report on sensory reactivity and anxiety
  • Perceived Causal Relations scale

Findings:

  • All reactivity types correlated with anxiety
  • Hyperreactivity seen as a cause
  • Seeking seen as an effect

Strengths:

  • First of its kind
  • Large sample size
  • Valid self-report measures

Limitations:

  • No control group
  • Bias potential
  • Did not assess other contributing factors

Conclusion:

  • Pioneering, insightful research
  • Informs future studies and treatments

Final Thoughts

Numerous studies explore the sensory-anxiety link in autism. These two studies are particularly useful for understanding newer research directions.

Most previous work focused solely on hyperreactivity; newer research explores a range of sensory responses.

More research is needed, but these insights are crucial for developing effective, personalised interventions.
